Compound Interest Calculator: Formula, Examples, and Step-by-Step Guide

This page teaches compound interest like a real teacher: clear definitions, correct formulas, worked examples, and a growth table so you can verify each step. Use it for savings, investments, and understanding loans.

Compound interest means your money grows not only on the original amount (called the principal) but also on the interest that was added earlier. In other words, when interest is added to your balance, that new balance becomes the base for the next calculation. Over time, this creates a “snowball” effect.

To quickly check results after you understand the concept, use this compound interest calculator to compute final amount, total interest, and year-by-year growth. But first, we’ll learn the math so the calculator output makes sense and you can spot mistakes instantly.

1) Key Terms (Understand These First)

  • Principal (P): the starting amount (example: ₹10,000).
  • Rate (r): annual interest rate as a decimal (10% = 0.10).
  • Time (t): total time in years.
  • Compounding frequency (n): how many times interest is added per year (yearly=1, quarterly=4, monthly=12).
  • Amount (A): final total after compounding.
  • Compound Interest (CI): total interest earned, calculated as CI = A − P.

2) Compound Interest Formula (Main Formula)

The standard compound interest amount formula is: 

A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t)

How to read it: “Amount equals principal times (1 plus rate divided by number of compounds) raised to (number of compounds times time).”

What each symbol means

  • P = principal (starting money)
  • r = annual interest rate in decimal form
  • n = number of compounding periods per year
  • t = years
  • A = final amount after interest

3) Worked Example (Yearly Compounding) — Teacher Style

Let’s say you invest ₹10,000 at 10% per year for 3 years, compounded yearly. So: P = 10000, r = 0.10, n = 1, t = 3.

Step 1: Write the formula

A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t)

Step 2: Substitute values

A = 10000 × (1 + 0.10/1)^(1×3)

Step 3: Simplify inside brackets and exponent

A = 10000 × (1.10)^3

Step 4: Compute the power

(1.10)^3 = 1.10 × 1.10 × 1.10 = 1.331

Step 5: Multiply by principal to get the amount

A = 10000 × 1.331 = ₹13,310

Step 6: Find compound interest only

CI = A − P = 13310 − 10000 = ₹3,310

4) Simple Interest vs Compound Interest (Clear Difference)

Simple interest calculates interest only on the principal: 

SI = P × r × t

For the same example: SI = 10000 × 0.10 × 3 = ₹3,000. But compound interest gave ₹3,310. The extra ₹310 happens because in year 2 and year 3, interest is calculated on a bigger amount (principal + previous interest).

5) Same Example, But Compounded Monthly

Now we compound monthly. The values: P = 10000, r = 0.10, t = 3, n = 12. 

A = 10000 × (1 + 0.10/12)^(12×3)
A = 10000 × (1 + 0.0083333)^(36)
A = 10000 × (1.0083333)^(36)

This power is not fun to do by hand, so a calculator is practical. Approximate result: 

A ≈ ₹13,489
CI ≈ ₹3,489

Monthly compounding is usually slightly higher than yearly because interest gets added sooner and starts earning interest earlier.

6) Growth Table (Year-by-Year Verification)

For yearly compounding at 10% with P = ₹10,000:

Year Starting Amount Interest (10%) Ending Amount
1 ₹10,000 ₹1,000 ₹11,000
2 ₹11,000 ₹1,100 ₹12,100
3 ₹12,100 ₹1,210 ₹13,310

Notice how the interest grows each year (₹1,000 → ₹1,100 → ₹1,210). That is compounding in action.

7) Common Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

  • Mistake 1: Using 10 instead of 0.10. Always convert percent to decimal: 10% = 0.10.
  • Mistake 2: Forgetting n×t in the exponent. The power must be (n×t), not just t.
  • Mistake 3: Mixing months and years. If time is in months, convert to years or adjust n correctly.
  • Mistake 4: Confusing A and CI. A is total amount, CI is only interest: CI = A − P.

8) Practical Tips (Real-Life Use)

Compound interest appears everywhere:

  • Savings & investments: long-term growth becomes powerful because compounding rewards time.
  • Loans: interest can compound too, especially when interest is added periodically.
  • Planning: small differences in rate or time create big differences after many years.

A simple rule: if you want compounding to help you, start early and stay consistent. If compounding is working against you (like credit card debt), pay it down quickly.

9) FAQ

How do I calculate only compound interest?

First compute A using the compound formula, then subtract principal: CI = A − P.

Does more frequent compounding always mean more money?

Usually yes, but the difference between monthly and daily may be small for short durations. Over long periods, it becomes more noticeable.

What if I add money every month?

Then we use a “future value of a series” formula. Many calculators support monthly contributions automatically.
